title: Too Much Work
date: 2017-07-25
description: Does such a thing really exist?
---

I was chatting with an intern the other day, and he asked about my experience at a former company. "Well," I said, "we didn't have enough people to cover every product, so ---"

"Ah," he interrupted, "too much work."

"Well, no," I replied.

The reason I disagreed is that I'm not sure there's such a thing as too much work. What there is, I'd argue, is too little willingness to make tough choices on what to cut and what to keep, too little back-and-forth about what is and isn't realistic, and too little transparency regarding what will and won't get done.

For example, given a list of 100 high priority tasks, I might only have time this month to complete five at a satisfactory level of quality. This simple breakdown *doesn't* mean that it will take me 20 months to complete all 100 tasks. Next month, new tasks will join that queue, and undoubtedly many of them will be higher priority than the remaining 95 tasks. I might take three years to get to the lower priority tasks, or I might never get to them.

In this situation, you might be tempted to call me overworked. But if I'm doing my job correctly, I'm still working standard hours and maintaining a solid balance between work and life. I have a credible explanation for why I'm working on those  five tasks and not the other 95. I have estimated delivery dates and a task or two that I can kick to the curb if something critical arises. I might even have a list of hiring criteria for expanding the team or an unorthodox plan for getting other groups involved. Most importantly, I'm broadcasting this information to all interested parties on a planning page --- *if I'm doing my job correctly*.

The X factor in this hypothetical situation is my hypothetical boss. Obviously it's trivial for a manager to assign unattainable deadlines to tasks and then scream when they don't get done. But such a situation isn't "too much work. " It's just "a bad manager."

I openly acknowledge that I've been lucky throughout my career, but I've yet to meet the manager who *isn't* open to having an honest conversation about what can be done in what amount of time and at what level of quality. These sorts of conversations can be contentious. They might even feel like personal attacks, but they force you to articulate your reasoning and present a compelling, comprehensive argument. Beyond being a fantastic mental exercise, they prepare you for your *next* job. Every rung up the corporate ladder is more about what the company should do and why. These conversations are practice sessions for your promotion.

Of course, these conversations can easily go from contentious to downright hostile. I can imagine managers flip-flopping every few days and prioritizing the newest problems over the most important ones --- or having no idea how to perform a task and naively assuming it's simple. Likewise, I can imagine individual contributors chafing at having to justify themselves so often or feeling like priorities change too quickly for them to accomplish anything of substance. Like so many other problems, however, the root of "too much work" *over the long term* is usually just a breakdown in communications.

Occasional *short term* crunches consisting of a day or two are different. These crunches are inevitable as you sprint towards a goal --- maybe even healthy. There's a satisfaction in working late one evening, shipping a product, and then leaving early on Friday. There's camaraderie in the shared stress of pushing something across the goal line. Like boxers hugging after a fight, stressful situations forge strong relationships. These relationships pay productivity dividends over time and strengthen a corporate culture.
