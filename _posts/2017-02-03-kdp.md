The Kindle Direct Publishing (KDP) process is a disaster. I wrote an article about it for Medium some time ago, and my tone was so negative that I deleted the post. Maybe I'll delete this one, too. &#9786;

I assumed I would gain perspective as the months passed and be able to assess KDP in a more balanced fashion. Unfortunately, time has failed to heal all wounds or even scar them over. A year later, I still look back on the process and struggle to fathom how it exists in its present form.

My frustration stems from two core points:

* There exists no reliable way to test your book prior to publication.
* There exists no reliable way to update your book after publication.

And Amazon is the *market leader*! They own the vast majority of the e-book market[^1]. If anyone should have an outstanding, streamlined publishing experience, it's Amazon.

The process:

1. Write a manuscript in [lightweight markup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightweight_markup_language).

2. Use a tool like [Pandoc](http://pandoc.org/) to convert the manuscript to [EPUB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EPUB) format.

3. Test the EPUB in the [Nook](http://www.nook.com/nookapp/) app, [iBooks](http://www.apple.com/ibooks/), or some other EPUB reader. It probably looks great.

4. Upload the EPUB to Amazon. Amazon converts the EPUB to a Kindle book.

5. Attempt to test the Kindle book in the online preview app. Discover that the preview app is inaccurate to the point of being useless.

6. Publish the book and hope for the best.

7. Purchase your own book, download to your device(s), and identify a bizarre formatting issue that didn't occur in the EPUB.

8. Modify your manuscript and repeat steps 2--7.

9. Try to verify your changes and fail, because once you purchase an e-book on Amazon, you own *that version* of the book forever.

So the online preview app doesn't work, and any updates you make only apply to new purchases. If your book contains a technical error or other issue, you can't provide customers with a corrected version---or even verify that your fix worked. The iBooks publishing process has neither of these issues. I haven't tried Barnes & Noble.

Ultimately, though, if you want to *sell* books rather than just publish them, you probably need to be on the Kindle Store. If you know of any accurate testing tools, please let me know on LinkedIn.
