A long time ago[^1], I read that expert [Go](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_(game)) players sometimes see a move, agree amongst themselves that it is an excellent one, and are then unable to articulate *why*. I have only the most rudimentary knowledge of Go; maybe the author overstated this phenomenon. True or not, I like the notion and think it relates to not just technical writing, but all product development.

![Go board](/assets/images/go.jpg)

In business, leadership wants *quantitative* metrics. They want numbers. The reason is straightforward: if your job is to make decisions and live with the consequences, you want all available information before deciding on a course of action. Numbers are concrete. They help compensate for your biases. They also provide you with a more credible defense in the result of failure. For technical writing, how many people are reading the documentation? How long are they spending on each page? How many support tickets are open? What percentage of closed support tickets included links to the documentation? How many documentation bugs are open? What is the average time to resolve a bug?

If a product's documentation only received eight page views last week, you're probably not interested in tasking a writer to improve it. Likewise, a product with 97 open support tickets probably warrants further examination. Numbers are valuable for guiding action. In aggregate, you might even be able to use them to demonstrate the value of a product.

Evaluating individual employees is trickier. If I provide a *service*, maybe you can measure the speed at which I perform that service and have some idea of how good I am at my job. Or maybe you need to balance that metric against the number of complaints received or number of repeat customers within a given timeframe. I'm oversimplifying, of course, but quantitative metrics still seem useful here---not the full story, but decent indicators.

Where quantitative metrics start to fall flat is in the evaluation of product-based employees---people who make things. If I make something, tangible or otherwise, how can you measure the process by which I created it? You need subject matter expertise (or at least some insight into the intended audience) to offer a credible opinion. In other words, you need to know how to play Go. This sort of knowledge can't be expressed through numbers; it's experience[^2]. Was the amount of time I took to make it reasonable? Does it reflect well on the company's brand? Is it unique and interesting? These are all subjective notions, better suited to essays than spreadsheets. Leadership might not be interested in an essay.

Hollywood defines success in terms of numbers---dollars, mostly---but a film performing poorly at the box office doesn't mean the director and cinematographer were bad at their jobs. Nor does it even mean the film was bad. Beautiful paintings sit unsold, important novels gather dust, and great music goes unheard. Still, the software industry persists in trying to distill the work of programmers, designers, and writers down to quantitative metrics, perhaps because these people create *products*, not art.

In product development, performance might regress for months before it improves. Tremendous amounts of important code might offer no visible benefit to the end user. Bugs might undermine a beautiful design, or terrible documentation might obscure the elegant simplicity of an API. Just like in film---or any collaborative medium---the product doesn't necessarily reflect the work of its creators.

Conversely, an application might feel snappy and responsive without actually being any faster than its competition. A design might look fresh and exciting. Documentation might read in a pitch-perfect, engaging voice. But we can't attach numbers to these qualities. Worse yet, if the work is sufficiently innovative, we might not know why these changes or additions make us feel elated or giddy or passionate or hopeful. Maybe they bring our vision of the product one step closer to reality, or maybe they redefine our vision of the product entirely. We might not be able to articulate *why*, but we know they're great moves.

I'm not trying to paint managers as villains or buffoons. After all, I'm a manager. I understand being busy, needing to *prove* value, and telling someone, "Just give me the bottom line." This article is as much a reminder to myself as it is a reminder to the rest of the world. As much as we might try to measure the success of a product against a series of metrics, we can't measure its contributors that way. To evaluate someone's performance, we have to understand the circumstances, the output, the audience, and the product. We need to be connected to the process. That sort of day to day engagement builds knowledge and trust and understanding, none of which, fortunately or unfortunately, fit on a bar graph.
