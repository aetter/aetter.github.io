title: Good vs. Great
date: 2017-06-08
description: How much more expensive is it to produce something top-shelf?
---

For years, my team and I produced documentation that I defined as "good." I don't mean the word as an insult. Every member of the team was (and remains) hard-working and brilliant, and I firmly believe most other software documentation teams would have produced something mediocre under similar circumstances. In this case, "good" was good enough. Our corporate mandate wasn't to create world-class documentation. Instead, we were to write what we could, edit what we couldn't, and try to maintain some semblance of a company-wide quality bar for all published materials. In the same way that a single landscaper couldn't possibly maintain a 200-acre property, the job was just too big for the team. We prioritized aggressively, defined our most critical job functions, and provided light coverage for a staggering number of projects.

These sorts of tight constraints force you to find creative solutions to problems. Our tools were open source, which saved money up front and let the entire company contribute content without additional licensing costs. We led writing workshops, wrote [whole books](https://www.amazon.com/Modern-Technical-Writing-Introduction-Documentation-ebook/dp/B01A2QL9SS) on how to approach software documentation, developed automated test suites, leveraged existing infrastructure, and tried to provide *guidance* more than *support*. The strategy worked. The documentation was good enough, even if we weren't writing most of it.

Over time, however, I began to consider how to create something not just good, but great. My initial thoughts were not encouraging. I estimated that I'd need twice as many writers, a copy editor, a part-time developer, and a design budget. A team of that size would likely need a second manager, as well. On the one hand, I knew that each element was critical to delivering something great. On the other hand, the wish list felt excessive after operating under such severe constraints for so long.

Upon further reflection, it wasn't excessive. In fact, it was in line with the cost of most physical goods. The multiplier in cost between "good" and "great" appears to be between 2–3x. You can get a Victorinox chef's knife for $45, but a Wusthof will set you back $129. The Honda Fit starts at $16,000, but a BMW 3 Series runs $34,000. Likewise, the latest iPad is $300, but a well-equipped Pro model costs $750.

But those are monetary examples. Some time-based examples of the amount of effort it takes to move a product from good to great:

- In [an interview with Kotaku](http://kotaku.com/5918831/starcraft-ii-heart-of-the-swarm-is-99-complete) in June 2012, the lead designer on a StarCraft II expansion said, ""We are 99% done, but that last 1%'s a bitch." The expansion limped out the door eight months later in March 2013.
- After publishing the first three books of a Song of Ice and Fire in four years, George R. R. Martin has been writing the Winds of Winter for [six years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire#Publishing_history). To be fair, each book is great in its own right, but concluding a long-running series in a satisfying way is monumentally more complex than crafting individual novels.
- Hell, decorating a cake often takes longer than mixing, baking, assembling, and frosting it.

Of course, the concept of diminishing returns is nothing new. You can represent it with a simple curve. Companies and individuals just need to decide where they want to sit on that curve. There's no wrong answer, but both parties need to be in agreement on the caliber of product they're trying to make. Executives often have too much pride to admit that their companies don't create the best products on the market, and middle managers often don't feel empowered to ask for the huge uptick in resources they need to deliver superlative outcomes.
