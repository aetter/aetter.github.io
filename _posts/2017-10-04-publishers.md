---
layout: post
title: Are Publishers Obsolete?
date: 2017-10-04
description: The relationship between middlemen and art.
---

When the internet became *a thing*, forward-thinking people everywhere realized that it had the power to kill entire businesses, especially the businesses of middlemen. If you weren't making something yourself, you were in danger of customers typing `www` and buying it directly from the creator. In this article, I don’t want to talk about physical goods. Instead, let's consider the middlemen who distribute art: record labels, film studios, video game publishers, and book publishers.

Despite the existence --- or "threat" --- of Napster and Spotify, the expense of recording studio albums and organizing world tours seems to have kept the record industry alive. Movies and television shows cost so much money to make that film studios continue to thrive, though independent works are flourishing, too. Similarly, video games are expensive to produce and often have ongoing server costs, but they too have a vibrant independent scene. In these three mediums, artists and companies seem poised to happily coexist for the foreseeable future.

Books, however, are different. They cost almost nothing to produce. You can write one on a used Chromebook with no negative impact on the quality of the final product. The manuscript for *War and Peace* only requires 3 MB of storage space. The amount of computing power required to generate an EPUB is trivial, and then you can simply upload the EPUB to Amazon, Barnes & Noble, or Apple. The entire process takes five minutes. In today's ecosystem, what benefits do publishers provide?

* **Curation** - Publishers decide which works they want to publish, based largely on which works they believe will generate revenue. Ultimately, though, their opinions are just opinions. If you think something is good, publish it. Every published author has tales of rejection letters. J.K. Rowling says "loads" of publishers passed on *Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone*.

* **Editing** - Publishers edit books. This service ensures that books are free of typos and grammatical issues, but it also makes books more marketable, cohesive, and just plain better. If you have no money, you can get a style guide from the library, self-proofread your book, and ask some smart friends for help. If you have some money, a professional editor might cost $60 per hour.

* **Cover design** - Publishers design covers. If you have no money, use a free cover design tool. The final result will be ugly, but serviceable. If you have some money, a professional designer probably wants several hundred dollars for a cover.

* **Formatting and creation** - Publishers perform the final formatting on a book, transforming it from a manuscript into something readable. For simple books, easy-to-use software can handle this task.

* **Pricing** - Publishers set prices. These prices are likely "market rates" that don't account for length or quality. If you write romance novels and have four more on the way, why not set the price for the first book at $6 to build an audience? You know better than anyone what you think is a fair price for your work.

* **Marketing** - Publishers market books. When is the last time marketing for a book reached you?

* **Distribution** - Publishers print books and ship them to stores. In the case of e-books, Amazon, Barnes & Noble, and Apple can handle distribution without publisher involvement.

* **Localization** - Publishers translate popular books into other languages. If your book makes enough money to warrant localization, you can pay one of dozens of companies to translate it.

Enumerated like this, it might seem like book publishers are doing a lot of work. And for physical books with complex layouts that need careful review from subject-matter experts (i.e. textbooks), they probably are. But for a novel, book of poetry, or memoir, the cost of a publisher is hard to justify. If you self-publish, you likely pocket 70% of the final purchase price for your book. The remaining 30% goes to the retailer. If you work with a publisher, you likely pocket 10% of the purchase price. You *might* receive a small advance.

Of all the services they provide, the real value-add of book publishers today boils down to only three things: editing, formatting, and distribution. And if you plan to publish an e-book, you only need that first service. My advice: find an editor you trust, skip the publisher, and retain control of your work.
