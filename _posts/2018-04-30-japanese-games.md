title: The Japanese Games Renaissance
date: 2018-04-30
description: What happened?
---

When I was growing up, Japan was the epicenter of game development. All my favorite games came from Japan: Final Fantasy, The Legend of Zelda, Punch-Out!!, Street Fighter II, Chrono Trigger, Mario Kart, Mega Man 2, Secret of Mana. I'm ignoring some gems like Doom and Sim City, but to me, "Western" game developers didn't start to catch up until the late 1990s, with releases like Baldur's Gate, StarCraft, Ultima Online, Goldeneye, and Unreal Tournament. Even then, Japanese developers were going blow-for-blow with their Western counterparts. Those same years included Gran Turismo, Castlevania: Symphony of the Night, Ocarina of Time, Final Fantasy VII, and Metal Gear Solid.

Then something happened. I'm not sure what, though I suspect it has to do with the licensing of two game engines: Unreal and Quake. Rather than viewing these engines as their own "secret sauce," Epic Games and id Software, the American companies behind the engines, licensed them to other game companies. Some of the best games of the late 1990s and early 2000s use these engines: Half-Life, Medal of Honor: Allied Assault, Call of Duty, and my favorite game of all-time, Deus Ex. Nowadays, of course, many --- perhaps even most --- console games use the Unreal engine.

Building on top of licensed (or open source) software has always paid dividends in terms of efficiency. I suspect that these efficiency gains allowed Western developers to get very good at exploring new ideas and iterating on existing ones. In the early 2000s, "middleware" like the Havoc physics engine and Bink Video became popular, further enhancing efficiency. For the most part, Japanese game developers continued to develop tools in-house and release games at a slower rate.

By the late 2000s, most of the great games on the market came from the West: Gears of War, Oblivion, BioShock, Portal, Mass Effect, Halo 3, Uncharted, Grand Theft Auto --- the list goes on. To be sure, Japanese developers produced some amazing titles in that span of time, but the list was dwindling. The only obvious standouts had Mario or Zelda in their names. As unfair as it is to compare the output of a single country to all of Europe and North America, the Japanese produced better content for 25 years. The 2000s were the turning point.

Then something else happened: the West saw dollar signs. With the [proliferation of high speed internet](http://www.nielsen.com/content/dam/corporate/us/en/newswire/uploads/2009/03/overview-of-home-internet-access-in-the-us-jan-6.pdf) and maturity of services like Xbox Live and the PlayStation Network, game developers realized that the $60 price on the box was just a starting point. The age of downloadable content (DLC) had arrived.

The Horse Armor Pack might be the most infamous example of DLC: for $2.50, your horse in Oblivion could have shiny Elven armor. The armor conferred no in-game benefits; it just looked cool. An intern probably whipped it up over the course of a few days. In a [2016 interview](http://www.ign.com/articles/2016/03/17/fallout-4-director-workshop-popularity-was-the-biggest-surprise-for-bethesda), Todd Howard, director and executive producer at Bethesda Game Studios, called it "one of the most popular [DLC packs] we've done, believe it or not." By today's standards, horse armor seems pretty benign, but at the time, many consumers were outraged.

Despite the outrage, the financial impact was significant. Developers loved DLC. Even better than asking players to pay for DLC, though, was getting them to pay a monthly subscription fee to a massively multiplayer online role-playing game (MMORPG). Chasing the ludicrous financial success of World of Warcraft, the late 2000s and early 2010s are littered with the digital carcasses of failed MMORPGs: Asheron's Call, City of Heroes, Dark Age of Camelot, Shadowbane, Star Trek Online, and Star Wars Galaxies, all of which came from American game developers.

Even if they didn't want to produce MMORPGs, Western game developers still pursued perpetual income. Call of Duty has followed an annual release cycle since 2005. Assassin's Creed has followed the same schedule since 2007, with only a single gap in 2016. The Battlefield series has been almost as reliable. And of course, they all had plenty of DLC.

Even more insidious is the type of DLC, though. If you offer outfits or other cosmetic enhancements, players can eventually buy them all. There's a ceiling on the amount of money a person can spend. More modern DLC consists of randomized content packs, sometimes called "loot boxes." These loot boxes contain *something*, maybe an assortment of outfits or cosmetic enhancements, but not necessarily the ones you wanted. But the next box might contain what you want. Or the next one. The ceiling disappears. Players can buy loot boxes indefinitely.

Today, subscription-based MMORPGs are generally regarded as unsustainable. Large publishers are instead pushing "games as a service." This description typically means an online game with content that is designed to be played again and again. The goal is to keep players engaged over a long period of time. Engaged players, you can imagine, purchase more loot boxes, and the long period of time gives developers a chance to create more DLC, leading to a virtuous cycle of money flowing out of customer bank accounts and into the outstretched arms of game publishers.

Except, well, for the games from Japan. The Japanese appear to have missed the memo about games as a service and predatory DLC. Instead, they've adopted the practices that made Western developers so successful in the early 2000s: use existing technology (in-house or licensed) rather than developing it from scratch for every new release, explore new ideas, iterate, and offer substantial, non-random DLC.

If you look at the best games of 2016, 2017, and 2018, almost all of them were developed in Japan or published by Sony: Zelda: Breath of the Wild, Mario Odyssey, Splatoon 2, Persona 5, Nier: Automata, Nioh, Fire Emblem Fates, Dark Souls III, Monster Hunter World, Ni No Kuni II, Uncharted 4, Horizon Zero Dawn, God of War, and Final Fantasy XV. Every major disappointment comes from a Western studio that focused more on post-sale revenue streams than the game itself: Destiny 2, Middle-earth: Shadow of War, Call of Duty: WWII, Sea of Thieves, Battlefield 1, Mass Effect: Andromeda, Star Wars: Battlefront II, Deus Ex: Mankind Divided, and Halo Wars 2, to name a few.

Most Western exceptions are independent games like Stardew Valley and Celeste, made by *one* and six people, respectively. Large, Western publishers[^1] continue to chase the trendy gravy train du jour rather than creating unique interactive art and assuming that success will follow. The current sentiment in the West seems to be that it's better to try and create the next Overwatch or League of Legends and go out of business within three years than it is to release an interesting experience, make a solid profit, and repeat.

And that's a real shame. I miss the amazing games that Bungie, Remedy, Blizzard, BioWare, Epic, Valve, Ubisoft, Eidos, Rare, Visceral, Maxis, and Origin used to produce. I want the development of games to be a stable, vibrant industry with beacons all over the globe. As of right now, the only titles on my wish list come from a handful of islands in the northern Pacific.

[^1]: Aside from CD Projekt and Bethesda.
